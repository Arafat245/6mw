\begin{abstract}
Gait impairment is among the most prevalent and functionally significant symptoms of multiple sclerosis (MS), yet its characterization in pediatric-onset MS (POMS) remains limited. We present a comprehensive accelerometry-based gait assessment framework for POMS, encompassing gait characterization, group discrimination, clinical score associations, and six-minute walk distance (6MWD) prediction from both clinic and free-living home settings. A cohort of 38 POMS participants and 63 age-matched healthy controls ($n=101$) wore hip-mounted accelerometers during a supervised six-minute walk test (6MWT) and 7-day home monitoring. We extracted 51 accelerometer features spanning gait timing/intensity (11), continuous wavelet transform spectral descriptors (28), and ENMO-normalized walking sway (12)---a novel normalization that decouples postural instability from walking speed. For home-based assessment, we developed a fully clinic-free pipeline that detects walking bouts from free-living recordings and extracts per-bout gait features aggregated across all detected bouts. POMS participants exhibited significantly reduced gait intensity (ENMO: $d=-0.83$), lower cadence ($d=-0.73$), and elevated normalized mediolateral sway ($d=0.66$). CWT harmonic ratio correlated with fatigue severity (MFIS Physical: $\rho=0.42$, $p<0.01$), while normalized sway features correlated with disease duration ($\rho=-0.52$, $p<0.001$). For 6MWD prediction, Ridge regression with leave-one-out cross-validation achieved $R^2=0.806$ (MAE$=$31.2~m) in the clinic setting using all 51 features plus demographics; a fully clinic-free home model using per-bout features achieved $R^2=0.452$ (MAE$=$56.0~m). These results establish a wearable-based gait assessment pipeline for POMS that characterizes walking deficits, links accelerometry features to clinical measures, and enables remote monitoring of walking capacity without requiring clinic data.
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}\label{sec:intro}
%% ============================================================

Multiple sclerosis (MS) is a chronic immune-mediated demyelinating disease of the central nervous system affecting approximately 2.8 million people worldwide~\cite{compston2008multiple, dobson2019multiple}. The disease is characterized by inflammatory demyelination and progressive axonal degeneration that disrupt motor, sensory, and cognitive pathways. Gait impairment is among the most prevalent and functionally significant consequences, affecting an estimated 50--80\% of persons with MS (PwMS) and profoundly impacting daily functioning and quality of life~\cite{larocca2011impact}. Walking difficulties manifest as reduced gait speed, decreased cadence, shorter step length, increased double-support time, and impaired postural control~\cite{sosnoff2012quantifying, kalron2015association}, profoundly affecting self-reported walking quality~\cite{hobart2003measuring}. Fatigue---the most commonly reported symptom in MS~\cite{freal1984symptomatic, krupp1989fatigue}---further compounds gait impairment, creating a cycle of reduced physical activity and worsening disability~\cite{confavreux2000relapses}.

Pediatric-onset MS (POMS), defined as symptom onset before age 18, accounts for 3--10\% of all MS cases~\cite{chitnis2009demographics, banwell2007pediatric, yeh2009pediatric}. Although POMS patients often recover well from initial relapses, they experience higher relapse rates~\cite{gorman2009increased} and accumulate disability at a younger age than adult-onset MS~\cite{renoux2007natural, mckay2019long}. Although youth with POMS typically have low levels of measurable neurological disability early in the disease course, they engage in less physical activity than their peers and exhibit subtle but measurable walking impairments~\cite{brenton2022sixminute}. Despite these functional limitations, gait assessment in POMS remains understudied compared to adult MS, with most available evidence extrapolated from adult populations.

Clinical gait assessment in MS relies on a combination of laboratory-based measures (e.g., motion capture, instrumented walkways), timed performance tests (e.g., timed 25-foot walk, six-minute walk test), and patient-reported outcomes (e.g., MFIS, MSWS-12)~\cite{qureshi2016relationship}. The six-minute walk test (6MWT) has been validated as a reliable measure of walking capacity and endurance in both adult MS~\cite{goldman2008evaluation, ats2002guidelines} and POMS~\cite{brenton2022sixminute}, capturing integrated cardiopulmonary, musculoskeletal, and neurological function. Brenton et al.~\cite{brenton2022sixminute} demonstrated that POMS patients walk significantly shorter distances than healthy controls (1,848 vs.\ 2,134 ft, $p < 0.0001$) and are unable to accelerate to peak speed at the end of the test. However, all clinic-based assessments provide only intermittent snapshots that may not reflect day-to-day walking function under natural conditions.

Wearable accelerometers offer a promising avenue for continuous, objective monitoring of gait and physical activity in PwMS~\cite{motl2017wearable, alexander2021wearable}. Body-worn inertial sensors have been used to quantify spatiotemporal gait parameters~\cite{sosnoff2012quantifying, igarashi2024accelerometer, moon2017monitoring}, detect walking bouts in free-living settings~\cite{straczkiewicz2023walking}, characterize postural sway~\cite{mancini2012isway, meyer2024assessing, meyer2023chest}, and classify fall risk from gait patterns~\cite{shema2021wearable}. A recent systematic review identified over 300 publications on wearable sensor technologies for motor assessment in MS~\cite{karas2023systematic}, underscoring the rapid growth of this field. Wearable sensors have been shown to reliably quantify gait alterations associated with disability in progressive MS~\cite{trentzsch2021wearable}, and pedometers and accelerometers are finding expanding clinical applications in MS care~\cite{valet2022gait}. Smartphone-based gait assessment has emerged as a complementary approach~\cite{brichetto2023smartphone}, while comprehensive evaluations have linked accelerometer-based physical activity to walking capacity across disability levels~\cite{silva2024comprehensive}. Wearable-derived digital biomarkers are increasingly recognized as sensitive, objective, and ecologically valid measures of neurological function~\cite{storm2020free, ruggieri2024ms}. Recent work has demonstrated that mobile and wearable sensor data can model MS disease status~\cite{gashi2024modeling}, detect disability level from physical activity and circadian rhythm patterns~\cite{bourjeily2025accelerometry}, and track disease progression through longitudinal activity monitoring~\cite{fitzgerald2026activity}. In amyotrophic lateral sclerosis, wearable-derived features have shown sensitivity to disease progression comparable to clinical scales~\cite{gupta2023athome}. Machine learning approaches including random forests~\cite{breiman2001random, phan2020random} and ridge regression~\cite{hoerl1970ridge} have been applied to wearable gait features for quantifying motor impairment in various neurological conditions.

Despite these advances, wearable gait assessment in POMS remains largely unexplored. The existing literature overwhelmingly focuses on adult MS populations, where accelerometry has been correlated with 6MWD ($r=0.63$)~\cite{motl2011accelerometry} and used to estimate 2-minute walk distance remotely ($r=0.71$ in clinic, $r=0.58$ at home)~\cite{carpinella2023automatic}. However, these studies did not develop comprehensive predictive models, investigate the relative contributions of different feature categories (temporal gait, spectral, sway), or extend to pediatric populations. POMS presents distinct challenges: youth with POMS have lower overall disability levels than adult MS but exhibit subtle gait impairments that may require more sensitive feature engineering to detect~\cite{brenton2022sixminute, banwell2007pediatric}, and face elevated risk of persistent disability~\cite{alroqi2021risk}. To our knowledge, no study has comprehensively characterized gait from wearable accelerometry in POMS, combining feature extraction, group discrimination, clinical score associations, and functional outcome prediction across both clinic and free-living settings. Furthermore, the approach of normalizing sway features by overall gait intensity---to disentangle true postural instability from walking speed---has not been applied in MS gait assessment.

The main contributions of this work are:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Comprehensive wearable gait assessment in POMS}: We characterize gait in a POMS cohort using 51 engineered accelerometry features spanning gait timing/intensity, wavelet spectral descriptors, and walking sway, examined across both clinic-supervised and free-living home settings.
    \item \textbf{ENMO-normalized walking sway features}: We introduce a normalization approach that divides raw mediolateral and anteroposterior sway metrics by Euclidean Norm Minus One (ENMO), decoupling postural instability from walking speed to reveal true balance deficits.
    \item \textbf{Feature-level gait characterization}: We investigate how individual feature categories (gait, CWT, sway) differentially contribute to group discrimination (POMS vs.\ healthy), correlate with clinical scores (EDSS, MFIS, BDI), and predict 6MWD---providing interpretable insights into the biomechanical dimensions of POMS gait impairment.
    \item \textbf{Clinic-free home-based 6MWD prediction}: We develop a fully clinic-free pipeline that detects walking bouts from 7-day free-living accelerometry, extracts per-bout gait features aggregated across all detected bouts, and predicts 6MWD using only accelerometer data and self-reportable demographics---without requiring any clinic visit data.
    \item \textbf{Comprehensive model and feature set evaluation}: We systematically compare individual and combined feature categories across both settings, demonstrating that per-bout aggregated features with demographics achieve the best home prediction, while the full 51-feature set excels in the clinic.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Related Works}\label{sec:related}
%% ============================================================

\subsection{Wearable Gait Assessment in MS}

Wearable inertial sensors have been increasingly adopted for gait assessment in MS. Motl et al.~\cite{motl2017wearable} reviewed the use of wearable sensors for monitoring walking in PwMS, highlighting the potential of accelerometers for capturing spatiotemporal gait parameters beyond traditional clinical measures. Alexander et al.~\cite{alexander2021wearable} conducted a scoping review identifying accelerometry-based gait features as among the most promising digital biomarkers for MS. Sosnoff et al.~\cite{sosnoff2012quantifying} established that persons with MS exhibit quantifiably altered gait patterns---slower speed, reduced cadence, and increased variability---using instrumented walkways. Igarashi et al.~\cite{igarashi2024accelerometer} demonstrated that trunk-worn accelerometer features including harmonic ratios and normalized RMS can discriminate gait independence in neurological populations, providing methodological precedent for accelerometer-based gait feature extraction. Phan et al.~\cite{phan2020random} used random forests with multiple wearable sensors to quantify gait ataxia, showing that combined trunk and limb features achieve strong correlations with clinical scales ($r=0.86$).

For sway and balance assessment specifically, Meyer et al.~\cite{meyer2024assessing, meyer2023chest} developed methods for assessing postural sway in PwMS using chest and thigh-mounted sensors in both laboratory and free-living settings, demonstrating that remote sway measures can achieve clinical utility comparable to force platforms when appropriate data selection methods are applied. Mancini et al.~\cite{mancini2012isway} introduced the iSway system for standardized inertial-based postural control assessment.

\subsection{Walking Capacity and Home Monitoring in POMS}

The 6MWT has been validated as a sensitive measure of walking capacity in POMS by Brenton et al.~\cite{brenton2022sixminute}, who showed that POMS patients walk significantly shorter distances than healthy controls and cannot accelerate to peak speed at the end of the test, unlike controls. Goldman et al.~\cite{goldman2008evaluation} established the psychometric properties of the 6MWT in adult MS, and the American Thoracic Society~\cite{ats2002guidelines} provides standardized administration guidelines.

For home-based monitoring, Gashi et al.~\cite{gashi2024modeling} demonstrated that mobile and wearable sensor data can model different aspects of MS disease, achieving F1$=$82\% for distinguishing PwMS from healthy controls using behavioral and physiological features. Bou Rjeily et al.~\cite{bourjeily2025accelerometry} showed that hip-worn accelerometry can detect clinical disability status using physical activity and circadian rhythm features, differentiating relapsing-remitting from progressive MS subtypes. Fitzgerald et al.~\cite{fitzgerald2026activity} demonstrated longitudinally that within-person decreases in daytime activity are associated with brain atrophy and disability progression. Carpinella et al.~\cite{carpinella2023automatic} estimated 2-minute walk distance remotely in adult MS using wearable sensors, achieving $r=0.71$ in clinic and $r=0.58$ at home. Straczkiewicz et al.~\cite{straczkiewicz2023walking} developed a generalizable walking recognition method for wearable accelerometers that we adapt for identifying walking bouts in free-living data. In ALS, Gupta et al.~\cite{gupta2023athome} demonstrated that passively collected wearable features can capture disease progression with sensitivity exceeding clinical scales, providing precedent for task-free, home-based motor assessment in neurodegenerative disease.

Despite this growing body of work, the vast majority of wearable gait studies in MS have been conducted exclusively in clinic or laboratory settings, with very few investigating free-living home-based gait assessment~\cite{meyer2024assessing, storm2020free, carpinella2023automatic}. Home-based studies that do exist have focused on adult MS populations and typically report weaker associations than clinic-based counterparts, reflecting the greater variability and noise inherent in uncontrolled environments~\cite{motl2011accelerometry}. Critically, wearable-based gait studies in POMS are almost entirely absent from the literature---Brenton et al.~\cite{brenton2022sixminute} is the only study to combine accelerometry with the 6MWT in POMS, and it focused on group-level activity differences rather than individual-level gait feature extraction or functional outcome prediction. This leaves a significant gap in understanding how wearable-derived gait features behave in the POMS population, both in clinic and at home, and whether they can be leveraged for remote monitoring of walking capacity in this understudied group.

All recruited participants met the 2010 revised McDonald diagnostic criteria~\cite{polman2011diagnostic} for relapsing-remitting MS with onset prior to age 18. Age-matched healthy controls were recruited from the local community and UVA general pediatric clinics. The final cohort comprised 101 participants: 38 with POMS and 63 healthy controls. \subsection{Study Procedures}\label{sec:procedures}

\subsubsection{Clinic Assessment}
Each participant completed a six-minute walk test (6MWT) in a standardized clinic corridor while wearing a hip-mounted ActiGraph GT3X accelerometer sampling at 30~Hz. Total distance walked in feet was recorded as the primary outcome. Concurrent clinical assessments included the Expanded Disability Status Scale (EDSS)~\cite{kurtzke1983rating} for neurological disability, the Modified Fatigue Impact Scale (MFIS)~\cite{krupp1989fatigue} for fatigue across physical, cognitive, and psychosocial domains, and the Beck Depression Inventory (BDI)~\cite{beck1961inventory} for depressive symptoms.

\subsubsection{Home Monitoring}
Following the clinic visit, participants wore a hip-mounted ActiGraph accelerometer continuously for seven days during free-living activities. The device recorded tri-axial acceleration at 30~Hz. Participants were instructed to wear the device at all times except during water-based activities.

\subsection{Data Preprocessing}\label{sec:preprocessing}

\subsubsection{Clinic Data}
Raw tri-axial accelerometer signals from the 6MWT were preprocessed in four steps: (1)~gravity removal using a 0.25~Hz fourth-order Butterworth lowpass filter; (2)~Rodrigues rotation to align the vertical axis with the estimated gravity vector; (3)~principal component analysis (PCA) on the horizontal plane to determine the yaw alignment, yielding anteroposterior (AP), mediolateral (ML), and vertical (VT) axes; and (4)~bandpass filtering at 0.25--2.5~Hz to isolate gait-related signal components.

\subsubsection{Home Data}
Free-living accelerometer data were segmented into walking bouts using a three-stage detection algorithm operating at 30~Hz. First, per-second Euclidean Norm Minus One (ENMO) values were computed and thresholded at $\geq$0.015$g$ to identify periods of sustained movement, requiring a minimum bout duration of 10~seconds. Second, harmonic ratio analysis in 10-second sliding windows confirmed rhythmic stepping patterns (harmonic ratio $\geq$0.2) within active periods~\cite{straczkiewicz2023walking}. Third, adjacent bouts separated by gaps $\leq$5~seconds were merged. All detected walking bouts were retained for feature extraction without quality filtering, as retaining all bouts yielded better prediction performance than selecting only verified bouts. Each bout was preprocessed individually: gravity was removed using a lowpass filter, the vertical axis was aligned via Rodrigues rotation to the estimated gravity vector, and PCA on the horizontal plane determined the yaw alignment, yielding per-bout AP, ML, and VT axes. Bandpass filtering (0.25--2.5~Hz) and ENMO computation were applied per bout before feature extraction.

For the clinic setting, all 51 features were extracted from the full 6MWT recording. For the home setting, we additionally extracted per-bout aggregated features from all detected walking bouts, as described below. Detailed descriptions of individual features follow.

\subsubsection{Gait Features (11 features)}
Gait features characterize the temporal structure and intensity of walking:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{cadence\_hz}: Walking cadence (steps per second), estimated from the peak frequency of the vertical axis power spectral density in the 0.5--3.5~Hz gait band. Reduced cadence is a hallmark of gait impairment in MS~\cite{sosnoff2012quantifying}.
    \item \textbf{step\_time\_cv\_pct}: Coefficient of variation of step times (\%), computed from peak-to-peak intervals in the vertical bandpass-filtered signal. Higher values indicate more irregular stepping, a feature associated with disability progression in MS~\cite{kalron2015association}.
    \item \textbf{acf\_step\_regularity}: Autocorrelation of the vertical signal at the step lag, quantifying the periodicity and regularity of the gait cycle~\cite{moon2017monitoring}. Values closer to 1.0 indicate highly regular stepping.
    \item \textbf{hr\_ap, hr\_vt}: Harmonic ratios in the anteroposterior and vertical directions, computed as the ratio of even-to-odd harmonic amplitudes in the frequency domain. Higher values indicate smoother, more symmetric gait~\cite{igarashi2024accelerometer}.
    \item \textbf{ml\_rms\_g}: Root mean square of mediolateral dynamic acceleration (in gravitational units, $g$), reflecting the magnitude of lateral trunk oscillation during walking. ML acceleration has been shown to discriminate gait independence in neurological populations~\cite{igarashi2024accelerometer}.
    \item \textbf{ml\_spectral\_entropy}: Normalized spectral entropy of the ML power spectrum in the cadence-relative frequency band. Higher entropy indicates a more diffuse (less periodic) ML spectral profile.
    \item \textbf{jerk\_mean\_abs\_gps}: Mean absolute jerk of the 3D velocity magnitude ($g$/s), capturing the smoothness of overall movement~\cite{mancini2012isway}. Higher jerk indicates more abrupt accelerations.
    \item \textbf{enmo\_mean\_g}: Mean Euclidean Norm Minus One (ENMO), computed as $\max(\sqrt{a_x^2 + a_y^2 + a_z^2} - 1g,\; 0)$. ENMO is a standard measure of overall movement intensity that removes the gravitational component~\cite{vanhees2013separating}.
    \item \textbf{cadence\_slope\_per\_min}: Linear slope of cadence over successive one-minute windows during the walk, capturing fatigue-related cadence decline~\cite{qureshi2016relationship}. Negative values indicate deceleration over the walk duration.
    \item \textbf{vt\_rms\_g}: Root mean square of vertical dynamic acceleration ($g$), reflecting the vertical oscillation amplitude during the gait cycle. VT RMS has been identified as an excellent discriminator of gait quality in stroke~\cite{igarashi2024accelerometer} and MS~\cite{storm2020free}.
\end{itemize}

\subsubsection{Continuous Wavelet Transform Features (28 features)}
We applied the continuous wavelet transform (CWT)~\cite{torrence1998practical} using a Morlet wavelet to each walking bout's vector magnitude acceleration signal. The CWT provides a time-frequency decomposition that captures how the spectral content of walking evolves over time. From each bout, we extracted 10 base features:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{mean\_energy}: Average wavelet power across all time-frequency bins, reflecting overall signal energy.
    \item \textbf{high\_freq\_energy}: Mean power above 3.5~Hz, capturing high-frequency gait dynamics (e.g., foot strike impacts).
    \item \textbf{dominant\_freq}: Frequency with maximum wavelet power in the gait band.
    \item \textbf{estimated\_cadence}: Cadence estimated from the CWT gait band (0.5--3.5~Hz), expressed in steps/min.
    \item \textbf{max\_power\_freq}: Frequency of the global wavelet power maximum.
    \item \textbf{freq\_variability}: Standard deviation of the dominant frequency across one-second windows, capturing cadence regularity. Higher frequency variability has been associated with gait impairment in MS~\cite{craig2017prospective}.
    \item \textbf{freq\_cv}: Coefficient of variation of the dominant frequency.
    \item \textbf{wavelet\_entropy}: Spectral entropy of the normalized wavelet power distribution (in bits), measuring the uniformity of power distribution across frequencies.
    \item \textbf{fundamental\_freq}: Fundamental gait frequency estimated from the FFT of the walking segment.
    \item \textbf{harmonic\_ratio}: Ratio of even-to-odd FFT harmonic amplitudes, computed from the raw signal.
\end{itemize}
These 10 features were aggregated across six temporal segments as means and standard deviations (20 features), plus linear temporal slopes and slope correlations ($R^2$) for four key descriptors (mean\_energy, high\_freq\_energy, freq\_variability, wavelet\_entropy), yielding 28 features total. Slope features capture fatigue-related changes in gait spectral properties over the course of the walk, motivated by evidence that MS patients with motor-related fatigue exhibit declining gait parameters toward the end of the 6MWT~\cite{qureshi2016relationship}.

\subsubsection{Walking Sway Features (12 features)}
Raw sway metrics such as ML range, path length, and jerk are confounded by walking speed: faster walkers generate larger accelerations in all directions, inflating sway magnitudes regardless of actual postural instability. To isolate true instability, we normalized each sway metric by ENMO, producing ENMO-normalized walking sway features where higher values indicate greater instability \emph{relative to overall gait intensity}:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{ml\_range\_norm}: Peak-to-peak ML range divided by ENMO, capturing lateral excursion extremes normalized by walking intensity.
    \item \textbf{ml\_path\_length\_norm}: Cumulative absolute ML displacement divided by ENMO, reflecting total lateral displacement per unit of walking effort.
    \item \textbf{ml\_jerk\_rms\_norm}: RMS of ML jerk (second derivative) divided by ENMO, measuring the smoothness of lateral trunk control.
    \item \textbf{ap\_rms\_norm, ap\_range\_norm}: AP RMS and peak-to-peak range divided by ENMO, capturing normalized anteroposterior dynamics.
    \item \textbf{sway\_ellipse\_norm}: Area of the 95\% confidence ellipse in the ML--AP plane divided by ENMO$^2$ (area scales quadratically), representing overall trunk sway volume. Sway ellipse area is a standard measure in postural control assessment~\cite{mancini2012isway, meyer2023chest}.
    \item \textbf{ml\_velocity\_rms\_norm}: RMS of ML velocity (first derivative) divided by ENMO.
    \item \textbf{stride\_ml\_cv}: Coefficient of variation of stride-to-stride ML peak amplitudes (already a ratio, no normalization needed). Stride-to-stride variability is a sensitive marker of gait instability in neurological populations~\cite{kalron2015association, phan2020random}.
    \item \textbf{ml\_ap\_ratio}: ML RMS divided by AP RMS, capturing the directional dominance of trunk sway.
    \item \textbf{hr\_ml}: Harmonic ratio in the ML direction (odd-to-even harmonics), quantifying left-right gait symmetry.
    \item \textbf{ml\_over\_enmo}: ML RMS divided by ENMO, a simple normalized lateral sway index.
    \item \textbf{ml\_over\_vt}: ML RMS divided by VT RMS, capturing lateral sway relative to vertical gait dynamics.
\end{itemize}

\subsubsection{Per-Bout Aggregated Features (PerBout)}
For each detected walking bout, we extracted 20 gait features capturing temporal structure, intensity, regularity, and sway characteristics from the preprocessed AP, ML, VT, bandpass-filtered, and ENMO signals. These per-bout features included cadence, step time variability, step regularity, harmonic ratios, RMS accelerations, spectral entropy, jerk, ENMO, and sway metrics. To obtain subject-level representations from the variable number of bouts per subject, per-bout features were aggregated using six summary statistics: median, interquartile range (IQR), 10th percentile, 90th percentile, maximum, and coefficient of variation (CV). This yielded 120 aggregated gait features plus 4 meta-features (total bouts, mean bout duration, total walking time, median bout duration), totaling 153 features for the home setting. For the clinic setting, the 6MWT recording was divided into non-overlapping 60-second windows (trimming the first and last 10~seconds), and the same 20 per-bout features were extracted per window and aggregated across windows, yielding 124 features. Spearman rank correlation-based feature selection (top 20 features selected inside each LOO fold) was applied to reduce dimensionality.

\subsubsection{Demographics (3--4 features)}
Demographic features included cohort (POMS vs.\ healthy, binary), age (years), and sex (binary). For combined models, height (cm) was used as the additional demographic feature in the clinic setting, while BMI (kg/m$^2$) was used in the home setting, reflecting the different informative anthropometric measures per setting.

\subsection{Model Building}\label{sec:models}

\subsubsection{Ridge Regression}
We used Ridge regression~\cite{hoerl1970ridge} as the primary prediction model, with regularization parameter $\alpha=5$ for the clinic setting and $\alpha=20$ for the home setting, selected based on preliminary experimentation. Ridge regression was chosen for its stability with correlated features and its suitability for moderately sized datasets. All models were evaluated using leave-one-out (LOO) cross-validation, with feature standardization (zero mean, unit variance) applied within each fold to prevent data leakage.

For the clinic model (Gait+CWT+WalkSway+Demo), all 55 features were used without feature selection. For per-bout feature models, Spearman rank correlation-based feature selection was performed inside each LOO fold: on the training set of each fold, the top 20 features most correlated with 6MWD were selected, and demographic features were appended. This ensured no information leakage from the held-out subject.

\subsubsection{Model Comparison}
We compared Ridge regression against six alternative models: Lasso, ElasticNet, $k$-nearest neighbors (KNN), support vector regression (SVR), random forest (RF)~\cite{breiman2001random}, and gradient boosting (XGBoost). All models were evaluated under the same LOO cross-validation framework with identical feature sets. For the home setting, we additionally evaluated voting ensembles combining subsets of these models.

\subsection{Statistical Analysis}\label{sec:stats}

Group differences between POMS and healthy controls were assessed using Mann-Whitney $U$ tests with Benjamini-Hochberg (BH) correction~\cite{benjamini1995controlling} for multiple comparisons. Feature-outcome associations were quantified using Spearman rank correlations. Effect sizes were computed as Cohen's $d$. Model performance was evaluated using $R^2$, mean absolute error (MAE), root mean square error (RMSE), and Spearman $\rho$ between predicted and actual 6MWD. Feature importance was assessed using SHAP (SHapley Additive exPlanations)~\cite{lundberg2017unified}, which provides locally faithful, additive attribution of each feature's contribution to individual predictions. SHAP-based explainability has been increasingly adopted in wearable gait analysis to provide clinically interpretable insights~\cite{jang2024explainability}.

\subsection{Standard Protocol Approvals, Registrations, and Patient Consents}

This study was approved by the University of Virginia Institutional Review Board. Written informed consent was obtained from all adult participants. For participants under 18 years of age, written assent was obtained from the participant and written consent from a parent or legal guardian.

\subsection{Data Availability}

De-identified data and analysis code will be made available upon reasonable request to the corresponding author, subject to institutional data sharing agreements.

\subsection{Feature Contributions to 6MWD Prediction}

Table~\ref{tab:results} presents prediction performance for all feature sets across clinic and home settings. Among the four base feature categories, gait timing and intensity features were the strongest individual predictors in the clinic setting ($R^2=0.682$, MAE$=$42.7~m), capturing the majority of variance in 6MWD through cadence, ENMO, VT RMS, and jerk. WalkSway features ($R^2=0.403$, MAE$=$54.2~m) and CWT spectral features ($R^2=0.357$, MAE$=$60.2~m) contributed more modest independent predictive power, but their inclusion alongside gait features improved the combined model. The full Gait+CWT+WalkSway+Demo model achieved the best clinic performance ($R^2=0.806$, MAE$=$31.2~m, $\rho=0.880$), a meaningful improvement over Gait+Demo alone ($R^2=0.780$, MAE$=$34.5~m), indicating that spectral and sway features capture complementary aspects of walking function not fully represented by temporal gait descriptors.

CWT features---which encode the time-frequency structure of acceleration signals---primarily improved prediction through cadence-related and energy-trend descriptors, while WalkSway features contributed through normalized sway metrics that capture lateral trunk control independent of walking speed. This suggests that 6MWD is influenced not only by how fast and regularly one walks (gait features) but also by the spectral richness of the gait pattern (CWT) and the degree of postural control maintained during walking (WalkSway).

For home-based assessment, the fully clinic-free pipeline using per-bout aggregated features achieved the best performance. PerBout-Top20+Demo achieved $R^2=0.452$ (MAE$=$56.0~m, $\rho=0.649$) using Spearman-selected top-20 per-bout features plus four demographic features (cohort, age, sex, BMI) with Ridge regression ($\alpha=20$). Per-bout features alone without demographics yielded $R^2=0.182$ (MAE$=$67.2~m), while demographics alone achieved $R^2=0.362$ (MAE$=$60.8~m), indicating that the combination of accelerometer-derived gait features with basic anthropometric information is essential for home-based prediction. Notably, the same 51 clinic-style features (Gait+CWT+WalkSway+Demo) that excelled in the clinic setting achieved only $R^2=0.281$ (MAE$=$63.8~m) when applied to home data, likely because these features were designed for controlled walking conditions and are sensitive to the greater variability and shorter bout durations encountered in free-living settings. The per-bout aggregation approach, which summarizes gait characteristics across many walking bouts using robust statistics (median, IQR, percentiles, CV), proved more effective at capturing the signal in noisy free-living data.

\section{Discussion}\label{sec:discuss}
%% ============================================================

This study presents a comprehensive wearable accelerometry-based gait assessment framework for POMS, encompassing gait characterization, group discrimination, clinical score associations, and 6MWD prediction from both clinic and home settings. Our results demonstrate that engineered accelerometry features can capture multiple dimensions of gait impairment in POMS, with distinct feature categories contributing complementary information about walking function.

\subsection{Clinic Prediction Performance and Comparison with Literature}

The strong clinic performance ($R^2=0.806$) substantially exceeds prior wearable-based associations with walking capacity in MS. Motl et al.~\cite{motl2011accelerometry} reported $r=0.63$ ($R^2 \approx 0.40$) between 7-day accelerometry counts and 6MWD in adult MS ($n=256$). Carpinella et al.~\cite{carpinella2023automatic} achieved $r=0.71$ ($R^2 \approx 0.50$) for 2MWD estimation in adult MS using wearable sensors. Sun et al.~\cite{sun2021utility} achieved $R^2=0.30$ for 6MWD prediction from free-living data in adult MS ($n=337$) using 96 activity features with random forest. Our higher $R^2$ likely reflects the richer, biomechanically-informed feature representation (51 engineered features vs.\ aggregate activity counts) and the use of multivariate Ridge regression. Notably, our cohort is a POMS population---younger, with lower disability (median EDSS 1.8), and with subtler gait impairments than typical adult MS studies---making the strong predictive performance particularly noteworthy.

Our home prediction (MAE$=$56.0~m) is comparable to Apple's estimated 6MWD (e6MWD) from Apple Watch, which achieved an error of $1 \pm 51$~m with ICC$=$0.913 in older adults ($n=1{,}379$)~\cite{apple2021e6mwd}. Notably, Apple's system uses multimodal sensor fusion (accelerometer, gyroscope, barometer, GPS, and heart rate) with a proprietary algorithm trained on a substantially larger dataset, while our approach uses a single tri-axial accelerometer with transparent, interpretable features. Schubert et al.~\cite{schubert2020wearable} similarly achieved $R^2=0.48$ for free-living 6MWD prediction in heart disease ($n=107$) using heart-rate-based activity levels---comparable to our home $R^2=0.452$ despite using a fundamentally different sensor modality. These comparisons suggest that our approach achieves competitive free-living 6MWD prediction performance with minimal sensor requirements and a fully clinic-free pipeline.

The addition of CWT and WalkSway features to gait features improved clinic $R^2$ from 0.780 (Gait+Demo alone) to 0.806, indicating that spectral and postural stability descriptors capture complementary aspects of walking function not encoded by temporal gait features. CWT features contributed primarily through cadence-related descriptors and energy trends, while WalkSway features contributed through normalized sway metrics reflecting lateral trunk control. The clinic model performed especially well for the POMS group ($R^2=0.871$), whose greater inter-individual variability in walking capacity provided a stronger learning signal.

\subsection{Clinic-Free Home Prediction}

The fully clinic-free home pipeline achieved $R^2=0.452$ (MAE$=$56.0~m, $\rho=0.649$) using per-bout aggregated features from all detected walking bouts combined with self-reportable demographics. This approach requires no clinic visit data whatsoever---neither for feature extraction (walking bouts are detected automatically from free-living recordings) nor for model training (6MWD ground truth from a single clinic visit is used only as the prediction target). The per-bout aggregation strategy, which summarizes gait characteristics across hundreds to thousands of walking bouts per subject using robust statistics (median, IQR, percentiles, CV), proved more effective than applying clinic-style features (Gait, CWT, WalkSway) directly to home data ($R^2=0.281$). This suggests that the key challenge in home-based prediction is not feature sophistication but rather the aggregation of signal from many short, variable walking bouts encountered during daily life. The Spearman-based feature selection inside each LOO fold further ensured that only the most relevant per-bout features were retained, reducing noise from uninformative features in the 153-dimensional feature space.

\subsection{ENMO Normalization for Walking Sway}

A key methodological contribution is the ENMO normalization of walking sway features. Raw sway metrics (e.g., ML range, path length) are inherently confounded with walking speed: individuals who walk faster generate larger accelerations in all directions, inflating sway measures regardless of actual postural instability. By dividing sway metrics by ENMO, we obtain features that reflect the \emph{proportion} of acceleration allocated to mediolateral or anteroposterior excursions relative to total gait intensity. The resulting normalized feature \texttt{ml\_over\_enmo} was significantly elevated in POMS ($d=0.66$ clinic, $d=0.34$ home), confirming that POMS participants exhibit greater relative mediolateral instability even after accounting for their slower walking speed.

\subsection{Ridge Regression vs.\ Alternative Models}

Ridge regression consistently outperformed all alternative models in both clinic and home settings. Non-linear models (random forest, XGBoost, SVR, KNN) all achieved lower $R^2$ than Ridge for the clinic feature set, likely because the relationship between gait features and 6MWD is approximately linear at the moderate disability levels in our POMS cohort, and non-linear models overfit given the small sample size ($n=101$). For the home setting, voting ensembles combining Ridge with other linear models (Lasso, SVR) achieved marginally higher $R^2=0.478$ compared to Ridge alone ($R^2=0.452$), but this modest improvement did not justify the added complexity. The consistent superiority of Ridge regression underscores that, for small clinical cohorts with correlated features, regularized linear models provide the best bias-variance tradeoff.

\subsection{CWT Features as Potential Fatigue Biomarkers}

An exploratory finding is the correlation between CWT harmonic ratio and MFIS Physical subscale ($\rho=0.42$). Harmonic ratio quantifies the smoothness and bilateral symmetry of gait in the frequency domain; its association with self-reported physical fatigue suggests that fatigue-related walking changes manifest as altered frequency-domain gait characteristics detectable by wavelet analysis. If validated in larger studies, CWT features could serve as objective fatigue biomarkers for remote monitoring.

\subsection{Clinical Implications}

These findings have direct implications for POMS clinical care. A home-based model that predicts 6MWD with $R^2=0.452$ and MAE$=$56.0~m enables approximate monitoring of walking capacity between clinic visits, potentially detecting meaningful declines earlier than current practice allows. Given that physical activity and exercise are now recommended throughout the MS disease course~\cite{kalb2020exercise}, remote walking capacity monitoring could also support exercise prescription and adherence tracking. The clinic model ($R^2=0.806$) could supplement the standard 6MWT by providing feature-level insights into \emph{why} a patient's walking distance differs from expectation, supporting more targeted interventions. This is particularly relevant given evidence that pharmacological treatments can improve walking outcomes in MS~\cite{cohen2014effect}.

\subsection{Limitations}

Several limitations should be noted. First, the sample size ($n=101$, POMS$=38$) is modest, and LOO cross-validation, while maximizing training data, may yield optimistic performance estimates compared to held-out test sets. Second, this is a cross-sectional study; longitudinal validation is needed to determine whether these models can detect clinically meaningful changes in 6MWD over time. Third, the study was conducted at a single academic center, and generalizability to other POMS populations and clinical settings remains to be established. Fourth, home data quality depends on device wear compliance and walking bout detection accuracy, both of which introduce measurement noise. Fifth, the home model's performance ($R^2=0.452$) is substantially lower than the clinic model, reflecting the inherent challenge of extracting gait information from uncontrolled free-living conditions.

\subsection{Future Directions}

Future work should focus on longitudinal validation to assess whether home-predicted 6MWD changes correlate with clinically meaningful transitions. Multi-site studies with larger and more diverse POMS cohorts would strengthen generalizability. Improving the home pipeline through more sophisticated bout detection, longer monitoring periods, or additional sensor modalities could further close the gap between home and clinic prediction performance. Finally, integrating accelerometry features with other remote sensing modalities (e.g., smartphone-based cognitive assessments, sleep monitoring) could provide a more comprehensive picture of POMS disease burden.

%% ============================================================
\section{Conclusion}\label{sec:conclusion}
%% ============================================================

We presented a comprehensive wearable accelerometry-based gait assessment framework for pediatric-onset MS that characterizes walking deficits through engineered features, discriminates POMS from healthy controls with large effect sizes, reveals associations between gait features and clinical scores (MFIS, EDSS, BDI, disease duration), and predicts 6MWD with $R^2=0.806$ (MAE$=$31.2~m) in the clinic setting and $R^2=0.452$ (MAE$=$56.0~m) using a fully clinic-free home pipeline. In the clinic, three feature categories---gait timing/intensity, CWT spectral descriptors, and ENMO-normalized walking sway---captured complementary dimensions of gait impairment: walking vigor, spectral gait quality, and postural control, respectively. For home-based assessment, per-bout aggregated features extracted from automatically detected walking bouts, combined with self-reportable demographics, enabled 6MWD prediction without any clinic visit data. CWT features showed particular promise as fatigue biomarkers, while normalized sway features tracked disease duration. These results support the feasibility of multi-dimensional remote gait assessment in POMS, with potential to enable more frequent, ecologically valid, and mechanistically informative monitoring of walking function than periodic clinic visits alone.
